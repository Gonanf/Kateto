# Risk analysis

Identify delivery risks, their likelihood and impact, and one practical mitigation for each. Keep assumptions visible and avoid claiming external verification that was not performed.

## DMAIC framework (Lean Six Sigma)

Use this structured approach for risk and process improvement:

1. **Define** — What is the goal? What does the stakeholder expect? What is in scope?
2. **Measure** — Collect current process data. How frequent is the risk? What is the baseline? Define how and how often data is gathered.
3. **Analyze** — Identify root causes, gaps, and their downstream effects. Separate symptoms from causes.
4. **Improve** — Present findings and prepare mitigations. Design the solution.
5. **Control** — Document the new process. Monitor that the fix holds. Learn from what was done.

## Risk identification

Risks fall into common categories:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| Schedule | Unrealistic timelines, dependency delays, resource unavailability |
| Budget | Cost overruns, scope creep, vendor price changes |
| Quality | Defect rate, insufficient testing, skill gaps |
| People | Illness, turnover, team conflict, motivation loss |
| Technical | Integration failures, performance issues, technology obsolescence |
| External | Regulatory changes, market shifts, supplier failure |

## Likelihood / Impact matrix

For each risk, rate:
- **Likelihood**: Rare / Unlikely / Possible / Likely / Almost certain
- **Impact**: Negligible / Minor / Moderate / Major / Catastrophic

Combine into action:
- **High × High** = must mitigate immediately
- **Low × Low** = accept and monitor
- **Mixed** = plan a contingency

## Mitigation strategies

- **Avoid** — Change the plan to eliminate the risk
- **Mitigate** — Reduce likelihood or impact
- **Transfer** — Move the risk (insurance, fixed-price contract)
- **Accept** — Acknowledge it, plan a contingency budget or timeline buffer

## Lean waste reduction

When analyzing processes, watch for the eight wastes:
1. Defects — rework, errors
2. Over-processing — unnecessary steps
3. Overproduction — building what isn't needed yet
4. Waiting — idle time between steps
5. Inventory — unfinished work piling up
6. Transportation — moving work between people/tools unnecessarily
7. Motion — context switching, searching for information
8. Unused talent — not leveraging team member skills

Attribute each waste to: poor documentation, unclear standards, misunderstood customer needs, communication gaps, or ineffective process design.
